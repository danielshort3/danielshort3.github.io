Research task: Best practices for Project Starfall player animations

You are researching 2D side-scroller player animation best practices for a browser-based action RPG prototype called Project Starfall. Do not implement code or generate final assets. Produce a source-backed research report that can later guide animation, sprite-sheet, and prompt-writing work in Codex.

Project context:
- Project Starfall is a 2D side-scrolling action RPG/MMORPG-style prototype.
- The intended visual identity is "Starlit Frontier Fantasy": clean luminous fantasy, cel-shaded sprite assets, crisp contour outlines, readable silhouettes, fallen-star magic, guild-town warmth, blue-and-gold Starfall motifs, runes, portals, lanterns, crystal inlays, and practical crafted materials.
- The game should not look copied from MapleStory or any existing game. MapleStory can be considered only as a high-level action-coverage reference where idle, run, jump, climb, attack, cast, buff, hit, and defeat all have distinct readable poses.
- The current direction uses compact readable characters, roughly 3.5 to 4 heads tall, with minimal face detail. Character identity should come mostly from equipment layers, weapon silhouettes, class VFX, portraits, and accent colors, not from highly unique base bodies.
- The goal is to make animations feel natural, responsive, consistent, and authored, not awkward, forced, generic, or blatantly AI-generated.

Current animation contract to keep in mind:
- Player runtime sheets use 160px frames.
- Current layout is 6 columns by 10 rows, 960x1600 total.
- Row order: idle, run, jump, fall, climb, basic, skill, party, hit, defeat.
- Equipment layers are expected to align to the same frame grid, baseline, and body anchors.
- Supported player actions include idle, run, jump, fall, climb, basic attack, skill/offensive cast, party/buff cast, hit reaction, and defeat.
- Class readability matters:
  - Fighter, Guardian, Berserker, and Duelist should read as melee.
  - Archer, Sniper, Trapper, and Beast Archer should read as bow/ranged.
  - Mage, Fire Mage, Rune Mage, and Storm Mage should read as staff/wand/focus casters.

Research questions:
1. What are the most important principles for natural 2D side-scroller player animation?
   Cover key poses, timing, spacing, silhouette, arcs, squash/stretch, anticipation, follow-through, overlap, staging, and readability at small gameplay scale.

2. How should game animation balance natural motion with responsiveness?
   Specifically research how short anticipation, action locks, cancel windows, recovery frames, and follow-through can make attacks feel weighty without creating input lag.

3. What makes a good run cycle for compact side-view RPG characters?
   Include recommended key poses, foot-contact/pass frames, arm-leg counter motion, vertical bounce, common frame counts, and common mistakes.

4. What are best practices for the following action rows?
   - Idle: subtle breathing, posture, hair/cloth motion, no foot sliding.
   - Run: contact/pass poses, body lean, readable stride, loop energy.
   - Jump: immediate lift/readiness, optional squash without delaying input.
   - Fall: bracing pose distinct from jump.
   - Climb: alternating hands/feet, readable ladder/rope interaction.
   - Basic melee attack: windup, contact/release, recovery, weapon arc.
   - Bow attack: draw, aim, release, recovery, arrow/hand clarity.
   - Magic attack: focus/staff involvement, cast shape, release, readable VFX.
   - Skill cast: stronger offensive action distinct from basic attack.
   - Party/buff cast: supportive channel pose distinct from offensive cast.
   - Hit: quick recoil that preserves silhouette and gameplay readability.
   - Defeat: clean collapse/slump without awkward anatomy or visual glitches.

5. How should animation be designed for equipment overlays?
   Research stable baselines, consistent body proportions, hand/head/feet anchors, weapon attachment points, layer ordering, and how to avoid body movement that makes future gear layers hard to align.

6. What makes AI-generated sprite animation look obviously AI-generated?
   Identify common issues such as inconsistent anatomy, changing clothing, drifting weapon shape, random shadows, scale jitter, broken hands/feet, face changes, outline inconsistency, texture noise, unreadable VFX, cell-border artifacts, and inconsistent baselines. Recommend prevention and review methods.

7. What review checklist should be used after generating or editing sprite sheets?
   Include checks at full size and runtime scale for silhouette readability, frame-to-frame continuity, foot sliding, baseline stability, loop smoothness, attack readability, class identity, no cropped limbs, no floating fragments, no guide/chroma pixels, no copied style, and equipment-layer compatibility.

8. What source-backed recommendations apply specifically to Project Starfall?
   Translate the research into concrete guidance for the 6-frame rows and Starfall style. Recommend which animations can stay subtle, which need stronger key poses, and how to preserve the Starlit Frontier Fantasy identity.

Preferred source quality:
- Prioritize game animation books, industry articles, animator talks, GDC-style talks, professional animator blogs, studio breakdowns, and reputable game development resources.
- Avoid relying on unsourced SEO articles unless they point to useful primary references.
- You may include examples from existing games, but discuss them as high-level animation principles only. Do not recommend copying character designs, exact frame poses, or copyrighted art.

Required output format:
1. Executive summary: 8 to 12 bullets of the most actionable findings.
2. Source notes: cited sources with short explanations of why each source is useful.
3. Animation principles: natural motion and responsiveness guidelines for 2D side-scrollers.
4. Action-by-action recommendations: idle, run, jump, fall, climb, basic, skill, party/buff, hit, defeat.
5. Starfall-specific guidance: how to apply the research while preserving Starlit Frontier Fantasy and the current 6x10 sheet contract.
6. AI-art risk checklist: what to avoid and how to detect/fix it.
7. Final production checklist: a practical pass/fail checklist for reviewing the next Project Starfall player animation iteration.
