# Resume Best Practices Research

Last updated: 2026-04-12  
Scope: current U.S. private-sector resume guidance, with extra attention to data analytics, business intelligence, and data science roles.

## How to read this document

- This is a synthesis of recent guidance from reputable sources, primarily university career centers plus a few large employer-facing career resources.
- When sources disagree, I call that out explicitly.
- When a recommendation is an interpretation for data-focused roles rather than a direct statement from one source, I mark it as an **inference**.

## Quick takeaways

- For your target roles, a clean, single-column, ATS-friendly one-page resume is still the strongest default unless your relevant experience grows meaningfully. Sources: [Berkeley], [FSU], [UCLA], [USC], [UB-SOM].
- Recruiters and hiring managers still want fast scanning: role, company, dates, tools, scope, and business impact should be obvious within seconds. Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [USC], [UB-SOM].
- Data-role resumes perform better when they show applied work, measurable outcomes, and concrete tools rather than generic claims about being analytical or data-driven. Sources: [Berkeley], [Harvard], [BYU-I].
- Projects matter more in data roles than in many other functions, but they need to look like real applied work, not filler coursework. Sources: [Berkeley], [Harvard], [BYU-I].

## 1. Layout and visual structure

- Use a simple, predictable, single-column layout with clear section headings.
  - Avoid decorative formats that make the document harder to scan.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [USC], [UB-SOM], [SFSU].

- Keep formatting readable and restrained.
  - Standard guidance is readable fonts such as Arial, Calibri, Cambria, Helvetica, or Times New Roman, typically around 10-12 pt for body text.
  - Keep enough white space between sections and entries so each role/project reads as a distinct block.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [USC], [UB-SOM], [SFSU].

- Use black or near-black text and minimal styling.
  - Bold is useful for names, headings, and select key information; heavy color or ornamental styling is not.
  - Sources: [UCLA], [USC], [SFSU].

- Keep margins reasonable.
  - Common guidance lands between 0.5" and 1"; Berkeley allows as low as 0.25" if the page still reads cleanly.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [USC].

- Make the first third of the page do the most work.
  - The resume should surface the most relevant information early because employers scan quickly.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [UB-SOM], [BYU-I].

- Keep bullets visually efficient.
  - Berkeley explicitly recommends short bullets that are easy to read and ideally 1-2 lines.
  - This aligns with recruiter scanning behavior and is a strong fit for your one-page resumes.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UB-SOM].

## 2. Experience block formatting

- Include the same core elements for every experience entry: job title, company, location, and dates.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [USC], [UB-SOM], [SIU].

- Keep dates consistent throughout the document and align them on the same margin.
  - UCLA specifically prefers dates on the same margin and notes right alignment as preferred.
  - Sources: [UCLA], [UB-SOM], [SIU].

- Use month + year, not just years, for experience dates.
  - Sources: [UCLA], [USC], [UB-SOM], [SIU].

- **Specific note for your resumes:** the most standard high-clarity format for your audience is:

```text
Business Analyst                              February 2024 – Present
Visit Grand Junction | Grand Junction, CO
```

  - Why: it front-loads the role title, keeps dates easy to scan, and preserves a standard two-line experience header.
  - Important nuance: official sources vary on whether company or title appears first, but they agree that the entry should be simple, consistent, and include title/company/location/dates.
  - **Inference from sources:** for recruiter scanning in data roles, title-first is the better default because the title communicates relevance faster than the company name.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [UB-SOM], [BYU-I], [SIU].

- If you right-align dates or locations, use actual tab stops, not fake spacing.
  - UB specifically warns that manual spacing can behave like columns and create parsing problems.
  - Sources: [UB-SOM].

## 3. ATS compatibility

- Use standard section headings like `Experience`, `Education`, `Projects`, `Skills`, and `Certifications`.
  - ATS systems look for predictable labels.
  - Sources: [USC], [UB-SOM], [SFSU], [SIU].

- Avoid headers, footers, tables, text boxes, graphics, photos, icons, and complex templates.
  - This is one of the most consistent recommendations across current guides.
  - Sources: [UCLA], [USC], [UB-SOM], [SFSU], [SIU].

- Keep your contact information in the body of the document, not in a Word header.
  - Sources: [USC], [UB-SOM], [SIU].

- Use standard bullets and straightforward punctuation.
  - Fancy symbols and visual gimmicks add risk without helping conversion.
  - Sources: [UCLA], [UB-SOM].

- Use full URLs for LinkedIn, portfolio, and website links if you want ATS systems to reliably read them.
  - UB is explicit that linked text alone can be missed; include the actual URL text.
  - Sources: [UB-SOM].

- Tailor keywords to each job description.
  - Pull role titles, tools, certifications, and domain language from the posting and incorporate them naturally where they are truthful.
  - Sources: [UCLA], [USC], [UB-SOM], [SFSU], [SIU].

- Do not rely on a generic master resume for actual applications.
  - A strong base resume is useful, but application resumes should be tailored.
  - Sources: [UCLA], [USC], [SFSU].

## 4. File format: where guidance conflicts

- There is no single universal rule on PDF vs. DOCX.
  - Berkeley prefers PDF for submitted resumes.
  - FSU says create in Word and submit as PDF.
  - USC says Word or PDF converted from Word is recommended.
  - SFSU warns that some ATS systems may not read PDF reliably.
  - Indeed says `.docx` is often the safest ATS fallback, but to follow the job posting.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [FSU], [USC], [SFSU], [Indeed].

- Best practical recommendation:
  - Keep a clean master in Word.
  - Export a PDF from Word.
  - Submit the format requested by the employer.
  - If the application instructions are silent and the system is a large ATS portal, having both a `.docx` and a PDF version ready is prudent.
  - If you use PDF, test it by copy-pasting the PDF text into a plain document or by checking the platform's parsed preview when possible.
  - **Inference from sources:** this is the safest combined approach because reputable sources conflict on preferred upload format, but they consistently agree that formatting must survive parsing.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [FSU], [USC], [UB-SOM], [SFSU], [Indeed].

## 5. Resume length

- For most private-sector candidates with roughly 0-7 years of experience, one page remains the strongest default.
  - Berkeley explicitly says 0-7 years: keep it to one page.
  - UCLA and USC say one page is standard, with two pages acceptable when experience is more extensive.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [USC].

- If a second page exists, it should be because page one is already dense with highly relevant content, not because of loose writing or inefficient formatting.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [USC].

- For your current situation, one page is still the right target.
  - **Inference from sources:** your resume versions are audience-specific and not senior-executive resumes; a tight one-page format fits current expectations well.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [USC].

## 6. Content strategy and section ordering

- Your resume is a targeted marketing document, not a complete life history.
  - Several guides make this exact point in substance.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [SFSU], [SIU].

- Put the most relevant sections near the top.
  - Education can move down once experience becomes more relevant.
  - Projects can move up if they are a major reason you are qualified.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [FSU], [UCLA], [BYU-I].

- Reverse-chronological structure remains the safest default for private-sector hiring.
  - Sources: [UCLA], [UB-SOM], [SIU].

- Only include sections that strengthen your candidacy.
  - Good optional sections include Projects, Certifications, Relevant Coursework, Research, Leadership, and Volunteer work if relevant.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [UB-SOM].

## 7. Summary/profile/objective guidance

- A summary or profile is optional, not automatic.
  - It is most useful when changing careers, lacking directly relevant experience, or when you need to frame your brand quickly.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [FSU], [UB-SOM], [SIU].

- On a strong one-page, targeted resume, a summary often is not necessary.
  - FSU explicitly notes that on a one-page resume, which already functions as a summary, a summary/objective may not be needed.
  - Sources: [FSU], [SIU].

- If you do use a summary, avoid empty adjectives and generic claims.
  - Berkeley specifically warns against vague buzzwords and recommends concrete context instead.
  - Sources: [Berkeley].

- For your current resumes, removing the summary to free space for stronger experience and project content is consistent with current guidance.
  - **Inference from sources:** this is especially true because your target roles can be demonstrated through concrete bullets rather than a generic opener.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [FSU], [SIU].

## 8. Bullet writing best practices

- Write accomplishment-oriented bullets, not duty lists.
  - Sources: [UCLA], [USC], [UB-SOM], [SFSU].

- A strong bullet usually answers four questions:
  - What did you do?
  - How did you do it?
  - In what scale/context?
  - What changed because of it?
  - Sources: [UCLA], [UB-SOM].

- Start bullets with action verbs and avoid personal pronouns.
  - Sources: [FSU], [UCLA], [USC], [SFSU], [SIU].

- Quantify where possible using counts, percentages, dollars, time saved, volume, frequency, and scope.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [USC], [UB-SOM], [SFSU], [SIU].

- Use business impact, not just task completion.
  - Good examples: time saved, revenue influenced, cost avoided, process cycle reduced, accuracy improved, turnaround cut, stakeholder decisions supported, workload automated.
  - Sources: [UCLA], [USC], [UB-SOM].

- Keep bullets concise and high-signal.
  - Berkeley recommends short, easy-to-read bullets; UCLA and others emphasize readability and scan speed.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [UB-SOM].

## 9. Skills section best practices

- Include a skills section, but keep it focused and credible.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [UB-SOM], [BYU-I].

- Group technical skills logically where possible.
  - Example groups: Programming, BI/Visualization, Databases, ML/NLP, Cloud, Analytics/Statistics.
  - UCLA recommends categorizing relevant concrete skills; Berkeley provides domain-specific skill groupings for data science.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA].

- Only list skills you can actually use with little or no supervision, and be ready to defend them in an interview.
  - Sources: [UCLA], [BYU-I].

- Back up listed skills in experience/project bullets.
  - BYU-Idaho and UB both stress that skills gain credibility when they appear in evidence-based bullets rather than in a detached list.
  - Sources: [UB-SOM], [BYU-I].

- Avoid bloated skill lists that read like keyword dumping.
  - BYU-Idaho warns that long undifferentiated lists make it unclear what you truly know well.
  - Sources: [BYU-I].

- **Inference for your field:** for analytics/BI resumes, SQL, Excel, Tableau/Power BI, reporting automation, KPI analysis, experimentation/forecasting, and stakeholder communication usually deserve more emphasis than broad soft-skill labels. For data science versions, Python, model evaluation, NLP/ML frameworks, retrieval/modeling methods, and deployment/reproducibility evidence matter more.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [UB-SOM], [BYU-I].

## 10. Projects section best practices for data roles

- Projects are worth real space on data analytics, BI, and data science resumes when they prove applied capability.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [BYU-I].

- Strong project entries should show:
  - the purpose/problem,
  - the tools/methods used,
  - your role,
  - and the benefit or result.
  - Sources: [UCLA], [BYU-I].

- Prioritize original, applied, decision-relevant work over generic classroom assignments.
  - BYU-Idaho explicitly recommends tangible, creative, personally owned projects and warns against listing standard class assignments completed by everyone.
  - Sources: [BYU-I].

- For project-style entries, labeling the role clearly can help.
  - BYU-Idaho recommends role first, then company/organization, for relevant experience.
  - Sources: [BYU-I].

- **Inference for your field:** analytics/BI projects should read like business problem-solving, not just dashboard construction. Data science projects should include method + evaluation + outcome, not just model names.
  - Sources: [UCLA], [UB-SOM], [BYU-I].

- Do not include speculative or unbuilt projects as if they already exist.
  - **Inference from sources:** current guidance consistently emphasizes truthful, evidence-based, tailored content. A future idea should not displace a real project unless clearly labeled as ongoing.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [UB-SOM], [SFSU].

## 11. Education, coursework, certifications

- Include degree, institution, location, and graduation date.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [USC], [SIU].

- GPA is usually most useful if it is strong and you are early-career.
  - UCLA suggests including GPA if required or if above 3.0; SIU says 3.0+ and notes technical programs may require it.
  - Sources: [UCLA], [SIU].

- Relevant coursework is optional and should be selective.
  - Berkeley suggests 2-5 classes; SIU suggests 4-8 advanced courses if needed.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [SIU].

- Include relevant certifications and continuing education.
  - UCLA says certifications/licenses can be separate or placed under Education or Skills.
  - Berkeley says to include relevant continuing education or certifications.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA].

- For your audiences, certifications should appear on all versions if they support credibility and are current.
  - **Inference from sources:** if the certifications are relevant across analytics, data science, and tourism-facing analytics work, consistency is better than hiding them on only one version.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [UB-SOM].

## 12. Links, contact information, and personal details

- Put your name and contact info at the top of the page.
  - Sources: [FSU], [UCLA], [UB-SOM], [SIU].

- Include phone, professional email, city/state, and optionally LinkedIn and portfolio/website.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [UB-SOM], [SFSU].

- Keep the email and URL presentation professional.
  - Sources: [UB-SOM], [SFSU].

- Do not include photos, marital status, birth date, citizenship, or other unnecessary personal details on a U.S. private-sector resume.
  - Sources: [UCLA], [USC].

- Do not include references on the resume and do not write `References available upon request`.
  - Sources: [UCLA], [SFSU].

## 13. Tailoring for data analytics, BI, and data science

- Mirror the language of the job description where it is true.
  - If a posting says `SQL`, `Tableau`, `forecasting`, `A/B testing`, `Python`, `dbt`, `Power BI`, `Snowflake`, or `stakeholder reporting`, those exact terms should appear naturally when supported by your background.
  - Sources: [UCLA], [USC], [UB-SOM], [SFSU], [SIU].

- Translate technical work into decision impact.
  - In data analytics and BI, the hiring audience wants to know what decision improved, what process accelerated, what KPI moved, or what reporting became possible.
  - **Inference from sources:** this follows directly from the broad emphasis on accomplishments, quantified outcomes, and evidence of fit.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [UB-SOM], [BYU-I].

- Show tool depth without turning the resume into a software inventory.
  - The strongest resumes show tools in context, not just as a disconnected list.
  - Sources: [UB-SOM], [BYU-I].

- Make projects role-aligned.
  - For analytics/BI roles, favor dashboards, SQL analysis, automation, forecasting, experimentation, segmentation, reporting pipelines, and stakeholder-facing insights.
  - For data science roles, favor modeling, evaluation, retrieval/search, NLP, experimentation, data pipelines, and deployed or demo-able work.
  - **Inference from sources:** this is the practical application of general resume guidance plus official data-science-specific advice from Berkeley and BYU-Idaho.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [BYU-I].

## 14. Common mistakes to avoid

- Generic summaries full of adjectives and no evidence. Sources: [Berkeley], [FSU], [UB-SOM].
- Duty-based bullets with no scale or result. Sources: [UCLA], [USC], [UB-SOM].
- Overdesigned templates, columns, icons, tables, or header/footer content. Sources: [UCLA], [USC], [UB-SOM], [SFSU], [SIU].
- Untailored resumes that ignore the wording of the target job. Sources: [UCLA], [USC], [UB-SOM], [SFSU].
- Skill lists that are too long or unsupported by experience. Sources: [UB-SOM], [BYU-I].
- Irrelevant experience, stale coursework, or filler projects crowding out better evidence. Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [BYU-I], [SFSU].
- Broken or hidden hyperlinks, especially if the visible text is not the actual URL. Sources: [UB-SOM].
- File-format issues that change layout or break parsing. Sources: [Berkeley], [FSU], [UB-SOM], [SFSU], [Indeed].

## 15. Practical recommendations for your current resume work

- Keep using one-page, audience-specific versions.
  - This is consistent with current practice for targeted private-sector resumes in your experience range.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [USC].

- Use title-first, date-right experience headers and put company/location on the next line.
  - This is the clearest standard option for your resumes.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [UB-SOM], [SIU].

- Keep certifications on all versions if they are relevant and current.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [UB-SOM].

- Remove the summary unless a specific version needs it to explain a pivot.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [FSU], [UB-SOM], [SIU].

- Make every bullet earn space.
  - On your one-page versions, the strongest bullets are the ones that combine tool + scope + business effect.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [UB-SOM], [BYU-I].

- Keep projects demonstrable and audience-matched.
  - Real built projects should outrank future concepts.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [UCLA], [UB-SOM], [BYU-I].

- Preserve both `.docx` and `.pdf` outputs.
  - Follow employer instructions on which one to upload.
  - Sources: [Berkeley], [FSU], [USC], [UB-SOM], [SFSU], [Indeed].
